PROJECT JUPITER ACCOUNTABILITY REPORT

The $165 Billion Question: Who Decided, What They Promised, and Why It Matters

Evidence-Grade Public Report Doña Ana County, New Mexico Published: January 22, 2026 Status: FOR PUBLIC RELEASE

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

On September 19, 2025, four Doña Ana County Commissioners voted to approve the largest Industrial Revenue Bond in New Mexico history: $165 billion in tax breaks for Project Jupiter, a data center development proposed by BorderPlex Digital Assets LLC.

The vote was 4-1. The same four commissioners voted YES on three separate ordinances over four weeks, giving away:

  • $165 billion in IRB authorization (100% property tax abatement for 30 years)
  • $50 million in GRT tax share (LEDA incentive)
  • $4.5 million in reduced permit fees
In exchange, the county receives:
  • A fixed $12 million/year "PILOT" payment (far less than actual property tax value)
  • Promises of 750 jobs, 2,500 construction jobs, and $50 billion investment
  • Commitments on water usage, local hiring, and environmental standards
The problem: Many of those promises are not legally enforceable. The decision process was closed to the public until 2 days before the vote. The IRB application allegedly had "blank and missing pages." No economic impact analysis. No water study. No environmental review completed.

This report documents:

  • Who voted and why they're legally, politically, and administratively accountable
  • What contradictions exist between promises and enforceability
  • How the decision was made in private, with minimal public input
  • What you can do to demand accountability
Every claim in this report is backed by primary sources: legal documents, court filings, government records, and news coverage. We don't speculate. We document.

Open Meetings Act Violations Alleged

New Mexico's Open Meetings Act (NMSA § 10-15-1) requires:

  • Public business conducted in open meetings
  • Reasonable advance notice
  • Opportunity for public review of materials
What happened instead:
  • NDA signed in early 2025 (closed-door negotiations)
  • Private meetings held for months
  • Final documents released Sept 17, just 2 days before Sept 19 vote
  • Mayor of Sunland Park said he was "finding out information the same time as press releases"
  • IPRA requests for public records delayed or obstructed
Legal consequence: Violations of the Open Meetings Act are criminal misdemeanors (NMSA § 10-15-3). Actions taken in violation may be voidable. Source: Legal complaint; news coverage; Amended Motion for Stay

The Incomplete Application

The legal complaint filed October 17, 2025, alleges the IRB application contained "blank and missing pages" and lacked critical information required by statute.

New Mexico law (NMSA § 3-32-6) requires IRB applications to include financial projections, job creation data, and economic benefit analysis.

County Counsel told commissioners that jobs and revenue projections weren't required to be "specified in the application." But if the information wasn't in the application, how did commissioners verify the project's claims?

The county approved $165 billion—the largest IRB in state history—without complete information.

Source: Petition for Writ of Certiorari, filed Oct 17, 2025; Amended Motion for Stay

PART IV: THE MISSING ANALYSES

No Economic Impact Study

What's missing: Independent analysis comparing 30 years of property tax revenue (without abatement) to $12 million/year PILOT payment. Why it matters: Property taxes on a $50 billion (potentially $165 billion) project would be worth billions over 30 years. The county settled for $360 million ($12M x 30 years). Who got the better deal? Source: Absence documented in news coverage (KFOX14, Sept 19, 2025); PROJECT_JUPITER_ACCOUNTABILITY_MATRIX.csv row ISSUE-0030

No Water Impact Study

What's missing: Hydrological analysis of project's impact on Mesilla Basin aquifer. Why it matters: The basin is already water-stressed. Project plans to use up to 60,000 gallons/day (peak), with no disclosed monitoring or enforcement mechanism.

The state committed to subsidize brackish water drilling (State MOU lines 16-22), using public funds for private water use—but where's the public benefit analysis?

Source: Absence documented in PROJECT_JUPITER_ACCOUNTABILITY_MATRIX.csv row ISSUE-0033; no aquifer study released by county

No Environmental Review Completed

What's missing: Environmental assessment incomplete as of November 4, 2025 (expected within 6 months). Why it matters: Construction is scheduled for Q4 2025. The county approved permits before environmental review was complete. Contradiction: Environmental safeguards require review before construction, not during. Source: Amended Motion for Stay (environmental assessment status letter); County MOU Section 2.2 (construction timeline)

No Grid Impact Study

What's missing: Load analysis and grid interconnection study for Project Jupiter's microgrid. Why it matters: The MOU claims the microgrid "will not impact existing customers' power reliability or costs"—but based on what analysis? Source: County MOU Section 3.4.4 (claim); no supporting study disclosed

PART VIII: KEY FINDINGS

Finding 1: The Decision Was Made in Private

NDA, private meetings, and 2-day document release violated the spirit—and likely the letter—of New Mexico's Open Meetings Act.

Finding 2: The State Pre-Empted Local Control

Governor's Feb 25 MOU committed New Mexico 7 months before county vote, creating political pressure on commissioners.

Finding 3: The Application Was Incomplete

Legal complaint alleges "blank and missing pages" in IRB application. County approved $165 billion without complete information.

Finding 4: Key Promises Are Unenforceable

MOU Section 5.1 disclaims legal obligations for most commitments (jobs, investment, environmental claims). Only Appendix B contributions are binding.

Finding 5: Critical Analyses Were Absent

No economic impact study. No water impact study. No completed environmental review. Commissioners voted blind.

Finding 6: Natural Gas Is Not Zero Carbon

State MOU's claim that natural gas qualifies as "zero carbon" contradicts science, state law (HB93), and basic chemistry.

Finding 7: A 4-1 Voting Bloc Controlled the Outcome

Four commissioners voted identically three times. Susana Chaparro stood alone in opposition.

Finding 8: Retaliation Against Opposition Occurred

Commissioner Reynolds doxxed residents who opposed the project, then voted YES. Intimidation + support = evidence of personal stake or external pressure.

Finding 9: Documents Changed After Initial Release

Water caps and $6.9M contributions reportedly missing from early documents, added later. Version control failure or deliberate withholding.

Finding 10: The Public Was Excluded

Mayor of Sunland Park (adjacent municipality) excluded. Community members given 2 days to review documents. IPRA requests delayed or obstructed.
